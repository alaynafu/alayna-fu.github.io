Methodology

Methods

ChIPMOD models transcription through the actions of RNA polymerase II, focusing on initiation, elongation, and termination while testing different pausing mechanisms of the polymerase. Updating ChIPMOD to better represent the physical dynamics of RNAPII, we addressed several inconsistencies such as the binding of polymerase to the chromatin, processivity along the gene, and the termination of the polymerase on the gene. Additionally, we focused on the stochastic models of ChIPMOD, going from uniform randomness to a discrete probability distribution. After comparing results from the original and updated version of ChIPMOD to experimental data, we saw that the updated version better represented the data, yet we observed periodicity within the results that was found to be a numerical artifact within the algorithm. Deciding to start from scratch, we looked at initiation, elongation, and termination similar to ChIPMOD but with emphasis on more statistically representative reaction rates with the Gillespie algorithm along with position-specific parameters to directly match the experimental data. Out of all three models, the Gillespie model is observed to best match the experimental data but needs more work in parameter optimization.

Results

Comparing the plots created by the original ChIPMOD, the updated version, and the Gillespie model, we saw an increase in resemblance to the experimental data from recent findings2. In both the experimental data and in the graph created by the two updated models, we saw a peak in the density of the polymerase across multiple cells, alluding to the idea that promoter-proximal pausing increases the stability of the polymerase. With the Gillespie model, we saw that reaction rates calculated with the Gillespie algorithm and position-specific parameters were able to best represent the experimental data, as well as show how different sites on the gene directly affect the density of the polymerase.

Discussion

Transcription, when RNA polymerase transcribes RNA from DNA, is a fundamental step in gene expression where the genetic information in DNA is being read and carried out. In transcription, promoter-proximal pausing is a known regulatory step and appears in both sense and antisense strands of DNA, yet it is not widely understood. A better understanding of polymerase pausing with computational modeling could help researchers determine if promoter-proximal pausing is the defining factor between sense and antisense transcription and aid in the study of the chromatin state.
